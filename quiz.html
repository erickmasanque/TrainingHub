Comprehensive Exam & Exercises

Facilitating Learner-Centered Teaching: Modules 3-8

Module 3: Review of Developmental Theories

1. A Grade 3 student in a Bicol school is learning to weave a simple abaca pattern. She can't do it alone, but she succeeds with her grandmother guiding her hands. According to Vygotsky, this task is in the student's:

A. Zone of Achieved Development (ZAD)

B. Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)

C. Microsystem

D. Scaffolding Zone

Answer: B. Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)

2. During a group project, a teacher assigns a student who is skilled in video editing to teach his groupmates the basics. In this scenario, the skilled student is acting as the:

A. Scaffolder

B. Formal Operator

C. More Knowledgeable Other (MKO)

D. Zone of Proximal Development

Answer: C. More Knowledgeable Other (MKO)

3. A toddler at a Jollibee party sees a slice of cake and immediately tries to grab it, crying loudly when his mother stops him. According to Freud, this immediate demand for gratification is driven by the:

A. Ego

B. Superego

C. Conscience

D. Id

Answer: D. Id

4. A 5-year-old playing *taguan* (hide-and-seek) covers her own eyes and believes that because she can't see her playmates, they can't see her either. This is a classic example of what Piagetian concept?

A. Conservation

B. Object Permanence

C. Egocentrism

D. Formal Operational Thought

Answer: C. Egocentrism

5. A student decides not to copy his seatmate's answers on a quiz, not because he understands the school's honor code, but simply because he is afraid the teacher will catch him and give him a failing grade. This reasoning is characteristic of which of Kohlberg's levels?

A. Pre-conventional Level

B. Conventional Level

C. Post-conventional Level

D. Universal Principles Level

Answer: A. Pre-conventional Level

6. A child's development is strongly influenced by her daily interactions with her family, her teachers at her local daycare, and her friends at the barangay playground. According to Bronfenbrenner, these immediate settings are all part of the child's:

A. Mesosystem

B. Exosystem

C. Macrosystem

D. Microsystem

Answer: D. Microsystem

7. An infant whose *yaya* (nanny) consistently responds to her cries, feeds her on time, and provides comfort is likely to successfully resolve which of Erikson's psychosocial crises?

A. Autonomy vs. Shame/Doubt

B. Initiative vs. Guilt

C. Trust vs. Mistrust

D. Industry vs. Inferiority

Answer: C. Trust vs. Mistrust

8. A high school student in Manila is trying to figure out who she is by joining different clubs, changing her fashion style, and exploring various hobbies. Erikson would describe this stage as:

A. Intimacy vs. Isolation

B. Identity vs. Role Confusion

C. Generativity vs. Stagnation

D. Ego Integrity vs. Despair

Answer: B. Identity vs. Role Confusion

9. A teacher notices that her student's performance in school has declined. She later learns that the student's father lost his job at a factory in Laguna, causing stress at home. In Bronfenbrenner's model, the father's workplace is part of the child's:

A. Microsystem

B. Mesosystem

C. Exosystem

D. Macrosystem

Answer: C. Exosystem

10. A Grade 6 student understands that if you flatten a ball of clay, it's still the same amount of clay. This student is demonstrating the Piagetian principle of:

A. Object Permanence

B. Symbolic Thought

C. Conservation

D. Abstract Reasoning

Answer: C. Conservation

Module 4: Student Diversity

1. A teacher in a Quezon City high school has students from various provinces. To foster unity, she creates a project where groups present on their local festivals but then facilitates a discussion on the common themes of community and thanksgiving. This strategy primarily aims to:

A. Emphasize the differences between students to promote individuality.

B. Balance the celebration of diversity with the identification of unifying human experiences.

C. Suggest that one local culture is more vibrant than others.

D. Discourage students from sharing their personal backgrounds.

Answer: B. Balance the celebration of diversity with the identification of unifying human experiences.

2. A teacher in a public school in Makati notices that some students have the latest gadgets while others struggle with basic school supplies. This is an example of which factor of student diversity?

A. Learning style.

B. Exceptionalities.

C. Socioeconomic status.

D. Ethnic background.

Answer: C. Socioeconomic status.

3. To make a lesson on Philippine heroes more inclusive, a teacher includes figures from Mindanao and the Visayas, not just Luzon. This practice directly addresses the need to:

A. Focus only on the most famous historical figures.

B. Vary examples to reflect students' diverse backgrounds.

C. Assume all students share the same historical knowledge.

D. Prioritize a single narrative in history.

Answer: B. Vary examples to reflect students' diverse backgrounds.

4. A teacher purposely forms project groups with a mix of students: some who are quiet, some who are talkative, some who are artistic, and some who are organized. The primary benefit of this approach is:

A. It ensures that the most talkative students do all the work.

B. It makes classroom management more difficult for the teacher.

C. It exposes students to different perspectives and working styles, enhancing cognitive development.

D. It proves that students work best with people who are exactly like them.

Answer: C. It exposes students to different perspectives and working styles, enhancing cognitive development.

5. During a class discussion, a student shares a religious belief that is different from the majority of the class. What is the most appropriate action for the teacher?

A. Tell the student that their belief is incorrect.

B. Quickly change the topic to avoid disagreement.

C. Facilitate a respectful discussion, treating the student's perspective as a valid part of diversity.

D. Ask the student not to share religious beliefs in class.

Answer: C. Facilitate a respectful discussion, treating the student's perspective as a valid part of diversity.

6. A teacher allows students to demonstrate their understanding of a novel through various means: a written report, a dramatic monologue, or a comic strip. This approach primarily caters to diversity in:

A. Socioeconomic status.

B. Age and gender.

C. Learning styles and exceptionalities.

D. Geographic origin.

Answer: C. Learning styles and exceptionalities.

7. The Filipino cultural value of "pakikipagkapwa-tao" (shared identity and empathy) can be a powerful tool in a diverse classroom because it:

A. Encourages students to form exclusive groups.

B. Promotes a sense of shared humanity and understanding despite differences.

C. Focuses only on the needs of the individual, not the group.

D. Is only applicable in a homogenous, non-diverse setting.

Answer: B. Promotes a sense of shared humanity and understanding despite differences.

8. A teacher makes a consistent effort to learn how to pronounce the unique names of all her students correctly. This small but important action primarily shows:

A. That the teacher has a good memory.

B. Respect for the students' individual and cultural identities.

C. That the teacher favors students with unusual names.

D. That names are not an important part of a student's identity.

Answer: B. Respect for the students' individual and cultural identities.

9. A challenge to implementing differentiated instruction in many Philippine public schools is the large class size. An effective strategy to manage this is to:

A. Give up on differentiation and use a one-size-fits-all approach.

B. Focus only on the fast learners and let the others catch up.

C. Use flexible grouping strategies, where students can work in pairs, small groups, or individually.

D. Send all struggling students to a separate remedial class.

Answer: C. Use flexible grouping strategies, where students can work in pairs, small groups, or individually.

10. A teacher notices that students whose parents are OFWs have different perspectives on family compared to students whose parents work locally. In a class discussion, the teacher should:

A. Avoid the topic of family to prevent discomfort.

B. Conclude that one family structure is better than the other.

C. Use this as an opportunity to discuss different family dynamics as a valid aspect of diversity.

D. Only allow students from "traditional" families to share their experiences.

Answer: C. Use this as an opportunity to discuss different family dynamics as a valid aspect of diversity.

Module 5: Learning Styles & Multiple Intelligences

1. When teaching a new dance step for a Linggo ng Wika presentation, Teacher Ana first demonstrates the whole sequence, then breaks it down. This approach caters primarily to which type of learners?

A. Global learners who need to see the "big picture" before the parts.

B. Analytic learners who need to see the details first.

C. Auditory-symbolic learners who prefer written instructions.

D. Visual-iconic learners who only need to see the final pose.

Answer: A. Global learners who need to see the "big picture" before the parts.

2. A student in a Tarlac high school prefers to study with background music and often fidgets or walks around while memorizing. According to the brain hemisphere theory, these are characteristics of a:

A. Right-brain dominant (Global) learner.

B. Left-brain dominant (Analytic) learner.

C. Visual-symbolic learner.

D. Auditory-listener learner.

Answer: A. Right-brain dominant (Global) learner.

3. A teacher notices that one student excels at group work, easily understanding others' feelings and motivations, making her a natural leader. According to Howard Gardner, this student shows a high level of:

A. Interpersonal intelligence.

B. Intrapersonal intelligence.

C. Naturalist intelligence.

D. Bodily-kinesthetic intelligence.

Answer: A. Interpersonal intelligence.

4. A student learns the parts of a plant best by actually touching the leaves, stem, and roots, and planting a seed themselves. This indicates a preference for which sensory learning style?

A. Tactile/Kinesthetic.

B. Visual-iconic.

C. Auditory.

D. Visual-symbolic.

Answer: A. Tactile/Kinesthetic.

5. A teacher explains a mathematical formula on the board. One student understands it best by reading the formula (the symbols), while another understands it best by looking at a chart that shows how the formula is used. This illustrates the difference between:

A. Visual-symbolic and Visual-iconic learners.

B. Analytic and Global learners.

C. Auditory listeners and Auditory talkers.

D. Interpersonal and Intrapersonal intelligence.

Answer: A. Visual-symbolic and Visual-iconic learners.

6. A student from a family of musicians in Manila easily picks up rhythms and patterns in poetry and even in mathematical sequences. This student likely has a strong:

A. Musical intelligence.

B. Bodily-kinesthetic intelligence.

C. Naturalist intelligence.

D. Spatial intelligence.

Answer: A. Musical intelligence.

7. An analytic, left-brain dominant learner would most likely prefer which of the following activities when studying for an exam?

A. Creating a detailed, sequential outline of the topics.

B. Creating a mind map with colorful drawings.

C. Discussing the topics with a study group.

D. Walking around the room while reading their notes aloud.

Answer: A. Creating a detailed, sequential outline of the topics.

8. A student is highly self-aware, understands their own strengths and weaknesses, and prefers to work alone on projects that allow for self-reflection. This is a hallmark of:

A. Intrapersonal intelligence.

B. Interpersonal intelligence.

C. Existential intelligence.

D. Verbal-linguistic intelligence.

Answer: A. Intrapersonal intelligence.

9. A teacher in Palawan notices a student is exceptionally good at identifying different types of local plants and animals and understanding their roles in the ecosystem. This student demonstrates a high level of:

A. Naturalist intelligence.

B. Spatial intelligence.

C. Bodily-kinesthetic intelligence.

D. Logical-mathematical intelligence.

Answer: A. Naturalist intelligence.

10. A history teacher in Iloilo wants to teach about the Cry of Pugad Lawin. He offers students the choice to either write a reflective essay, create a diorama of the event, compose a song, or participate in a class debate. This assessment strategy is an excellent example of:

A. Differentiating assessment to cater to Multiple Intelligences.

B. A standardized testing method.

C. Focusing only on verbal-linguistic intelligence.

D. A summative assessment with a single correct output.

Answer: A. Differentiating assessment to cater to Multiple Intelligences.

Module 6: Learners with Exceptionalities

1. The new school building in a Taguig elementary school does not have ramps, only stairs. For a student who uses a wheelchair, the flight of stairs represents a:

A. Disability, because it is a physical object.

B. Handicap, because it is an environmental barrier that creates a disadvantage.

C. Learning disability, because it affects their ability to get to class.

D. An impairment that the student must overcome on their own.

Answer: B. Handicap, because it is an environmental barrier that creates a disadvantage.

2. A student has difficulty reading and writing despite having average intelligence and no sensory impairments. This student may have a specific:

A. Physical disability.

B. Learning disability.

C. Emotional disorder.

D. Speech impairment.

Answer: B. Learning disability.

3. A teacher in a public school in Pasig is preparing her classroom for a student with a visual impairment. Which of the following is the LEAST helpful accommodation?

A. Providing audio recordings of the lessons.

B. Speaking more slowly and loudly to the student.

C. Using large-print worksheets.

D. Ensuring the classroom is free of clutter and obstacles.

Answer: B. Speaking more slowly and loudly to the student.

4. A Grade 3 student is exceptionally advanced in mathematics, often finishing his work far ahead of his peers and showing a deep understanding of complex concepts. This student can be categorized as:

A. Having a learning disability in other subjects.

B. Gifted and talented.

C. Having ADHD.

D. A student with a behavioral disorder.

Answer: B. Gifted and talented.

5. Which of the following statements is an example of NOT using People-First Language?

A. "The student with a hearing impairment will sit in the front."

B. "The disabled boy in my class needs an assistant."

C. "We need to provide support for children with disabilities."

D. "He is a person who has epilepsy."

Answer: B. "The disabled boy in my class needs an assistant."

6. A student with Autism Spectrum Disorder thrives in a classroom that is predictable and has clear routines. A sudden change in the class schedule without warning is likely to cause the student:

A. Happiness and excitement.

B. Anxiety and distress.

C. A desire to lead the new activity.

D. No reaction at all.

Answer: B. Anxiety and distress.

7. A student who has difficulty focusing, is constantly in motion, and often blurts out answers in class may be showing signs of:

A. A hearing impairment.

B. Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD).

C. Giftedness.

D. A learning disability.

Answer: B. Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD).

8. The 1987 Philippine Constitution provides for the education of citizens with disabilities. This reflects a societal commitment to:

A. Segregating students with disabilities from mainstream schools.

B. Upholding the right to education for all citizens.

C. Providing charity for a disadvantaged group.

D. Limiting educational opportunities to those without disabilities.

Answer: B. Upholding the right to education for all citizens.

9. A student has a severe speech impairment, making it difficult for him to communicate verbally. An effective teaching strategy would be to:

A. Avoid calling on him in class to prevent embarrassment.

B. Work with him to use an alternative communication method, like a tablet or picture board.

C. Insist that he speaks in complete sentences like everyone else.

D. Assume he does not understand the lesson because he cannot speak clearly.

Answer: B. Work with him to use an alternative communication method, like a tablet or picture board.

10. A teacher is describing a new student to her colleagues. Which of the following statements best demonstrates the use of People-First Language?

A. "My new student is an autistic."

B. "I have a student who has autism spectrum disorder."

C. "The autistic boy in my class is very quiet."

D. "She's one of my special needs kids."

Answer: B. "I have a student who has autism spectrum disorder."

Module 7: Behaviorism

1. Every time the ice cream truck's bell rings down a street in Sampaloc, a young child feels excited and asks for money. The bell, which was once a neutral sound, now elicits excitement. This is a real-world example of:

A. Operant conditioning, because the child operates on the environment.

B. Thorndike's Law of Effect, because the consequence is positive.

C. Classical conditioning, where a neutral stimulus is paired with an unconditioned stimulus.

D. Skinner's theory of shaping behavior.

Answer: C. Classical conditioning, where a neutral stimulus is paired with an unconditioned stimulus.

2. A teacher wants to help a very shy student participate more in class. She starts by praising the student for just looking up, then for raising her hand, and eventually for speaking. This technique of reinforcing successive approximations of a target behavior is called:

A. Extinction.

B. Stimulus generalization.

C. Shaping.

D. Classical conditioning.

Answer: C. Shaping.

3. A student cleans his part of the classroom to avoid being scolded by the teacher. The removal of the unpleasant stimulus (the scolding) strengthens the cleaning behavior. This is an example of:

A. Positive reinforcement.

B. Punishment.

C. Negative reinforcement.

D. Extinction.

Answer: C. Negative reinforcement.

4. A student who was bitten by a large Aspin dog in his barangay now feels fear when he sees any large dog, not just Aspins. This is an example of:

A. Stimulus discrimination.

B. Spontaneous recovery.

C. Stimulus generalization.

D. Higher-order conditioning.

Answer: C. Stimulus generalization.

5. According to Thorndike's Law of Effect, a student will be more likely to repeat a behavior, such as studying for a test, if it is followed by:

A. A difficult and confusing lecture.

B. A neutral consequence that has no effect.

C. A pleasant or satisfying consequence, like a good grade.

D. An annoying consequence, like more homework.

Answer: C. A pleasant or satisfying consequence, like a good grade.

6. A teacher stops giving stickers for perfect attendance. Over time, students' perfect attendance rates begin to drop. This process is known as:

A. Shaping.

B. Generalization.

C. Extinction.

D. Reinforcement.

Answer: C. Extinction.

7. A student is not allowed to join his friends for recess because he did not finish his seatwork. The teacher is using which operant conditioning principle?

A. Positive reinforcement.

B. Negative reinforcement.

C. Punishment.

D. The Law of Readiness.

Answer: C. Punishment.

8. A teacher wants to start a new lesson on fractions, but she notices the students are tired and restless from their previous PE class. According to Thorndike's Law of Readiness, what should she do?

A. Proceed with the lesson as planned to stick to the schedule.

B. Scold the students for not being ready to learn.

C. Do a short, calming activity first to help the students become ready to learn.

D. Give them a surprise quiz on fractions immediately.

Answer: C. Do a short, calming activity first to help the students become ready to learn.

9. The key difference between classical and operant conditioning is that:

A. Classical conditioning involves voluntary behaviors, while operant conditioning involves involuntary reflexes.

B. Classical conditioning was developed by Skinner, and operant conditioning was developed by Pavlov.

C. Classical conditioning associates two stimuli, while operant conditioning associates a behavior with a consequence.

D. There is no significant difference between the two.

Answer: C. Classical conditioning associates two stimuli, while operant conditioning associates a behavior with a consequence.

10. A teacher wants to encourage her Grade 2 students to read more books. She tells them that for every 5 books they finish, they get a sticker. This reward system is based on which schedule of reinforcement?

A. Fixed-Interval, because the time is set.

B. Variable-Interval, because the time is unpredictable.

C. Fixed-Ratio, because reinforcement is given after a specific number of responses.

D. Variable-Ratio, because the number of books is unpredictable.

Answer: C. Fixed-Ratio, because reinforcement is given after a specific number of responses.

Module 8: Neo-Behaviorism

1. A student learns the route from her house to the new community library by walking with her mother every day. She never has to go alone until one day her mother asks her to return a book. The student is able to go to the library by herself without any problem. This is an example of:

A. Goal-directed behavior.

B. Observational learning.

C. Positive reinforcement.

D. Latent learning.

Answer: D. Latent learning.

2. A senior high school student in Cebu wants to become a nurse. She studies hard, joins a pre-med club, and volunteers at the local health center. According to Tolman, her actions are not just responses to stimuli but are examples of:

A. Latent learning.

B. A cognitive map.

C. Vicarious reinforcement.

D. Purposive, goal-directed behavior.

Answer: D. Purposive, goal-directed behavior.

3. A young boy watches his older brother get praised by their parents for cleaning his room. The next day, the young boy cleans his own room. According to Bandura, the younger brother's behavior was influenced by:

A. Latent learning.

B. An intervening variable.

C. A cognitive map.

D. Vicarious reinforcement.

Answer: D. Vicarious reinforcement.

4. A student knows all the rules for a basketball game from watching the PBA, but he cannot play well himself because he has not practiced shooting. According to Bandura's conditions for effective modeling, which condition is lacking?

A. Attention

B. Retention

C. Motivation

D. Motor Reproduction

Answer: D. Motor Reproduction

5. A jeepney driver in Manila knows many different shortcuts to avoid traffic. When his usual route is blocked, he easily finds an alternative way to his destination. Tolman would say the driver is using a:

A. Reinforcement schedule.

B. Goal-directed plan.

C. Social learning model.

D. Cognitive map.

Answer: D. Cognitive map.

6. A teacher tells her students, "If you finish your seatwork quietly, you will have 10 extra minutes for recess." The students' expectation of a reward influences their behavior. This expectation is an example of what Tolman called an:

A. Observable stimulus.

B. Modeled behavior.

C. Conditioned response.

D. Intervening variable.

Answer: D. Intervening variable.

7. A student learns how to be respectful and polite in class by observing how the teacher praises other students who say "po" and "opo." This is a clear example of:

A. Tolman's purposive behaviorism.

B. Classical conditioning.

C. Latent learning without performance.

D. Bandura's social learning theory (observational learning).

Answer: D. Bandura's social learning theory (observational learning).

8. A student watches a documentary about the importance of recycling for a class project. According to Bandura, the student has learned about recycling, even if she doesn't start recycling at home immediately. This illustrates the principle of:

A. Motor reproduction.

B. Reciprocal causation.

C. Vicarious punishment.

D. Learning without performance.

Answer: D. Learning without performance.

9. A student who is normally quiet and shy becomes more outgoing and participative when she is with her talkative and confident group of friends. Her friends' behavior influences her, and her participation, in turn, energizes the group. Bandura would call this dynamic interaction between person, behavior, and environment:

A. Modeling.

B. Cognitive mapping.

C. Latent learning.

D. Reciprocal causation.

Answer: D. Reciprocal causation.

10. A teacher wants her students to learn how to properly format a business letter. Instead of just explaining it, she provides a perfectly formatted sample letter for them to follow. The sample letter is serving as a:

A. Live model.

B. Verbal model.

C. Cognitive map.

D. Symbolic model.

Answer: D. Symbolic model.

Exercise: People-First Language

1. Original: "We are raising money for the handicapped."

A. ...for people who are handicapped.

B. ...for people with disabilities.

C. ...for the differently-abled.

Answer: B. ...for people with disabilities.
Reason: This focuses on the person, not the label. "Disability" is a more direct and respectful term than "handicapped" or euphemisms like "differently-abled."

2. Original: "He is an autistic student."

A. He is a student who has autism.

B. He is an autistic.

C. He is a student suffering from autism.

Answer: A. He is a student who has autism.
Reason: This puts the person ("student") first and avoids language that implies suffering or defines the person by their condition.

3. Original: "She's wheelchair-bound."

A. She is confined to a wheelchair.

B. She is a person who uses a wheelchair.

C. She is a wheelchair case.

Answer: B. She is a person who uses a wheelchair.
Reason: This emphasizes the person and describes the wheelchair as a tool she uses, rather than something that confines or defines her.

4. Original: "The learning disabled kids should go to the resource room."

A. The special kids should go to the resource room.

B. The kids with learning disabilities should go to the resource room.

C. The slow learners should go to the resource room.

Answer: B. The kids with learning disabilities should go to the resource room.
Reason: This uses People-First Language and avoids generic, often negative labels like "special" or "slow."

5. Original: "He is a victim of polio."

A. He is a person who had polio.

B. He is a polio victim.

C. He is afflicted with polio.

Answer: A. He is a person who had polio.
Reason: This avoids language of victimhood ("victim," "afflicted with") and simply states the fact of the person's medical history.

6. Original: "Normal students should be kind to them."

A. Regular students should be kind to them.

B. Students without disabilities should be kind to them.

C. Able-bodied students should be kind to them.

Answer: B. Students without disabilities should be kind to them.
Reason: "Normal" implies that people with disabilities are "abnormal." "Students without disabilities" is a neutral, factual descriptor.

7. Original: "She is mentally retarded."

A. She is a person with an intellectual disability.

B. She is mentally challenged.

C. She is slow.

Answer: A. She is a person with an intellectual disability.
Reason: "Intellectual disability" is the current, respectful term. "Mentally retarded" is an outdated and offensive clinical term.

8. Original: "The deaf man came to the office."

A. The hearing-impaired man came to the office.

B. The man who is deaf came to the office.

C. The man who can't hear came to the office.

Answer: B. The man who is deaf came to the office.
Reason: This puts the person first. While "Deaf" is an acceptable identifier in the Deaf community, People-First Language structure is preferred in general contexts.

9. Original: "This classroom is for the emotionally disturbed."

A. This classroom is for students with emotional or behavioral disorders.

B. This classroom is for students with issues.

C. This classroom is for the problem children.

Answer: A. This classroom is for students with emotional or behavioral disorders.
Reason: This uses specific, non-judgmental language and puts the person ("students") first.

10. Original: "He looks normal, but he has a disability."

A. He is a person with a non-visible disability.

B. He is a person who doesn't appear disabled.

C. He is a person with a hidden disability.

Answer: A. He is a person with a non-visible disability.
Reason: "Non-visible disability" is a neutral and respectful term that avoids implying that disabilities have a certain "look" or that the person is not "normal."
